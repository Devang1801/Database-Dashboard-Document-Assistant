Chapter 1 ‚Äî Stock Market Basics
The stock market is a regulated marketplace where buyers and sellers trade shares of publicly listed
companies. It serves as a barometer of economic health and a vehicle for wealth creation over time.
What Is a Stock?
A stock (or share) represents a unit of ownership in a corporation. Stockholders are entitled to a proportional
claim on the company's assets and earnings. Stocks are broadly classified into:
 Common Stock ‚Äî carries voting rights and variable dividends
 Preferred Stock ‚Äî fixed dividends, priority in liquidation, limited voting rights
 Growth Stocks ‚Äî high reinvestment, low/no dividends, capital appreciation focus
 Dividend Stocks ‚Äî regular income distributions to shareholders
How Stock Markets Work
Markets operate through a continuous auction mechanism. Buyers post bids; sellers post asks. A trade occurs
when bid ‚â• ask. Electronic order-matching engines at exchanges execute millions of such trades per second.

Chapter 3 ‚Äî Country Regulations
Every country's stock market operates under its own legal and regulatory framework designed to protect
investors, ensure fair trading, and maintain market integrity.
India ‚Äî SEBI
Securities and Exchange Board of India regulates all exchanges. Key rules: Insider Trading Regulations 2015,
LODR (Listing Obligations & Disclosure Requirements), T+1 settlement cycle.
USA ‚Äî SEC
Securities and Exchange Commission enforces Dodd-Frank Act, Sarbanes-Oxley Act. FINRA oversees
broker-dealers. Regulation NMS governs equity trading.
UK ‚Äî FCA
Financial Conduct Authority under FCA Handbook. Post-Brexit adaptation of MiFID II rules. Senior Managers
Certification Regime (SMCR).
EU ‚Äî ESMA
European Securities and Markets Authority coordinates MiFID II / MiFIR across 27 nations. GDPR intersects
with financial data reporting.
China ‚Äî CSRC
China Securities Regulatory Commission. A-shares (domestic), B-shares, H-shares (HK listed). Stock Connect
programs link mainland with HK.
Japan ‚Äî FSA
Financial Services Agency. Tokyo Stock Exchange rules. Stewardship Code promotes institutional investor
engagement.
Universal Regulatory Themes
 Disclosure requirements ‚Äî mandatory filing of financials
 Anti-money laundering (AML) and KYC norms
 Circuit breakers to halt trading during extreme volatility
 Short-selling restrictions in crisis periods
 Cross-border cooperation via IOSCO

Chapter 5 ‚Äî Historical Market Events
Understanding historical market crises helps investors recognize warning signs, manage risk, and appreciate
market resilience over long periods.
1929 ‚Äî The Great Crash
Wall Street Crash triggered by excessive speculation and leverage. Dow fell ~89% from peak to trough. Led to
the Great Depression and establishment of the SEC in 1934.
1987 ‚Äî Black Monday
October 19: Dow fell 22.6% in a single day ‚Äî the largest one-day percentage drop ever. Caused by portfolio
insurance programs and automated selling cascades. Led to introduction of circuit breakers.
2000 ‚Äî Dot-com Bubble
NASDAQ rose 400% from 1995‚Äì2000 driven by internet euphoria. Collapsed 78% by 2002. ~$5 trillion in
market cap wiped out. Many unprofitable startups went bankrupt.
2008 ‚Äî Global Financial Crisis
Subprime mortgage collapse triggered systemic banking failure. Lehman Brothers bankruptcy. S&P; 500 fell
57%. TARP ($700B) bailout and global QE followed.
2010 ‚Äî Flash Crash
May 6: Dow plunged ~1,000 points in minutes then recovered. Caused by high-frequency trading feedback
loops. Led to new market circuit breaker rules.
2020 ‚Äî COVID-19 Crash
S&P; 500 fell 34% in 33 days ‚Äî fastest bear market ever. Massive stimulus ($5T+) drove one of the fastest
recoveries. Remote-work tech stocks soared.
Key Lessons
 Markets always recover given sufficient time
 Leverage amplifies both gains and losses catastrophically
 Regulatory responses shape future market structure
 Sentiment cycles between greed and fear predictably
 Diversification reduces but does not eliminate systemic risk

Chapter 7 ‚Äî SQL Analytics
SQL (Structured Query Language) is widely used in finance for querying market databases, calculating metrics,
and building trading dashboards.
Common Financial SQL Queries
Top Performing Stocks by Return
SELECT ticker, ((close_price - open_price) / open_price) * 100 AS pct_return
FROM daily_prices
WHERE trade_date = '2024-01-15'
ORDER BY pct_return DESC LIMIT 10;
52-Week High/Low
SELECT ticker,
MAX(high_price) AS week52_high,
MIN(low_price) AS week52_low
FROM daily_prices
WHERE trade_date >= CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '52 weeks'
GROUP BY ticker;
Moving Average (20-day)
SELECT ticker, trade_date,
AVG(close_price) OVER (
PARTITION BY ticker
ORDER BY trade_date
ROWS BETWEEN 19 PRECEDING AND CURRENT ROW
) AS ma_20
FROM daily_prices;
Sector Aggregation
SELECT sector, COUNT(*) AS stocks,
AVG(pe_ratio) AS avg_pe,
SUM(market_cap) AS total_market_cap
FROM company_fundamentals
GROUP BY sector
ORDER BY total_market_cap DESC;

Chapter 8 ‚Äî Investment Concepts
Sound investment requires understanding core frameworks that guide decision-making across asset classes,
time horizons, and market conditions.
Value Investing
Pioneered by Graham & Buffett. Buy stocks trading below intrinsic value (margin of safety). Focus on P/B, P/E,
free cash flow. Long-term buy-and-hold orientation.
Growth Investing
Target companies with above-average revenue/earnings growth. Typically higher P/E tolerated. Examples:
early Amazon, Google, Infosys.
Dividend Investing
Build portfolio of high-yield, consistent dividend payers. Reinvest dividends (DRIP) for compounding. Focus on
dividend coverage ratio and payout sustainability.
Index Investing
Passive strategy tracking benchmark indices. Low cost, diversified, market-return objective. Championed by
John Bogle (Vanguard).
Momentum Investing
Buy recent winners, sell recent losers. Based on persistence of price trends. Requires disciplined entry/exit
rules.
Contrarian Investing
Bet against prevailing market sentiment. Buy fear, sell greed. Requires patience and high conviction.
Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)
Invest fixed amount at regular intervals regardless of price. Reduces impact of volatility and removes
market-timing emotion.
Compounding
Einstein's 'eighth wonder' ‚Äî returns generate returns. Rs 1 lakh at 12% CAGR = Rs 17.4 lakh in 25 years.

Chapter 11 ‚Äî Technical Indicators
Technical analysis uses price, volume, and derived indicators to forecast future price movements. It assumes
all information is already reflected in the price.
Trend Indicators
Moving Average (MA)
SMA: average over N periods. EMA: exponentially weighted. Crossovers (Golden Cross / Death Cross) signal
trend changes.
MACD
Moving Average Convergence Divergence. MACD line = 12-EMA minus 26-EMA. Signal line = 9-EMA of
MACD. Histogram shows momentum.
Parabolic SAR
Trailing stop-and-reverse indicator. Dots below price = uptrend; dots above = downtrend.
Bollinger Bands
20-day SMA ¬± 2 standard deviations. Price touching upper band = overbought signal; lower band = oversold.
Momentum / Oscillators
RSI (Relative Strength Index)
0‚Äì100 scale. Above 70 = overbought; below 30 = oversold. Divergence with price is powerful signal.
Stochastic Oscillator
%K and %D lines. Compares close to range over N periods. Works well in ranging markets.
CCI (Commodity Channel Index)
Measures deviation from statistical mean. ¬±100 thresholds used for signals.
Volume-Weighted Average Price (VWAP)
Intraday benchmark. Institutional orders benchmarked to VWAP. Trading above = bullish.
Support & Resistance
 Horizontal levels ‚Äî price zones where buying/selling repeatedly emerges
 Trendlines ‚Äî connect swing highs (resistance) or lows (support)
 Fibonacci retracements ‚Äî 23.6%, 38.2%, 50%, 61.8% levels

Chapter 12 ‚Äî Macroeconomic Impacts
Macroeconomic forces drive broad market trends. Understanding their transmission mechanisms is essential
for top-down investment analysis.
Interest Rates
Central bank rate hikes increase discount rates, reducing equity valuations (DCF impact). Banks benefit; growth
stocks suffer. Rate cuts stimulate markets. Fed Funds Rate, RBI Repo Rate are key benchmarks.
Inflation
Moderate inflation (2‚Äì3%) is healthy. High inflation erodes purchasing power, triggers rate hikes. Real assets
(commodities, real estate, TIPS) hedge inflation. CPI, WPI, PCE are key measures.
GDP Growth
Positive GDP growth supports corporate earnings. Contraction (2+ quarters) = recession. Leading indicators:
PMI, consumer confidence, building permits.
Currency Exchange Rates
Weak domestic currency boosts exporters (IT, pharma in India) but increases import costs. DXY (US Dollar
Index) moves impact global risk appetite.
Unemployment Rate
Low unemployment signals economic strength but may trigger wage inflation. Non-Farm Payrolls (NFP) is the
most market-moving US data release.
Fiscal Policy
Government spending and taxation. Stimulus packages boost demand; austerity suppresses it. Fiscal deficit
levels affect bond yields and currency.
Global Trade & Geopolitics
Trade wars, tariffs, sanctions, and conflicts disrupt supply chains and commodity prices. Russia-Ukraine war
reshaped energy markets; US-China tensions shaped semiconductor supply.
Key Economic Calendars Events
 US Federal Reserve FOMC meetings (8 per year)
 RBI Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) ‚Äî bi-monthly
 US Non-Farm Payrolls ‚Äî first Friday of each month
 CPI/WPI data releases ‚Äî monthly
 Quarterly GDP estimates and revisions
 Corporate earnings seasons ‚Äî quarterly

Chapter 13 ‚Äî Portfolio Strategies
Portfolio strategy encompasses asset allocation, diversification, rebalancing, and the systematic translation of
investment thesis into holdings.
Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)
Harry Markowitz (1952). Efficient frontier ‚Äî maximize return for given risk level. Correlation between assets
drives diversification benefit. Mean-variance optimization.
Core-Satellite Approach
Core (60‚Äì80%): low-cost index funds. Satellite (20‚Äì40%): active picks, thematic ETFs, alternatives. Balances
stability with alpha potential.
60/40 Portfolio
60% equities, 40% bonds. Traditional balanced allocation. Bonds cushion equity drawdowns. Lower
effectiveness in recent rising-rate environments.
Factor Investing (Smart Beta)
Systematic exposure to proven return drivers: Value, Momentum, Quality, Low Volatility, Small Cap.
Rules-based, between active and passive.
All-Weather Portfolio (Ray Dalio)
30% equities, 40% long-term bonds, 15% intermediate bonds, 7.5% gold, 7.5% commodities. Designed to
perform across all economic environments.
Barbell Strategy (Nassim Taleb)
90% ultra-safe assets + 10% high-risk/high-reward bets. Avoid middle-risk assets. Protects against black swans
while preserving upside.
Thematic Investing
AI, clean energy, digital payments, aging demographics. Concentrated bets on structural mega-trends. Higher
conviction + higher risk.
Rebalancing
 Calendar rebalancing ‚Äî quarterly or annually regardless of drift
 Threshold rebalancing ‚Äî rebalance when allocation drifts ¬±5% from target
 Tax-efficient rebalancing ‚Äî use new contributions to rebalance before selling
 Rebalancing captures value from mean reversion in asset classes

Chapter 14 ‚Äî Market Psychology
Behavioral finance reveals that markets are driven not only by logic but by human emotions, cognitive biases,
and collective psychology. Understanding these helps investors avoid costly mistakes.
Loss Aversion
Losses feel ~2x more painful than equivalent gains feel good (Kahneman & Tversky). Leads to holding losers
too long and selling winners too early.
Confirmation Bias
Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs. Investors ignore contradictory data about their holdings.
Herd Behavior
Following the crowd. Creates bubbles (everyone buys) and crashes (everyone sells). FOMO (Fear of Missing
Out) and panic selling are manifestations.
Anchoring
Over-reliance on first piece of information (e.g., original purchase price). Prevents rational reassessment of
position.
Overconfidence Bias
Investors overestimate their analytical ability. Active traders underperform markets partly due to
overconfidence-driven excess trading.
Recency Bias
Overweighting recent events. After a crash, investors are overly fearful; after a bull run, overly optimistic.
Disposition Effect
Tendency to sell winners too early (lock in gains) and hold losers too long (avoid realizing loss).
Mental Accounting
Treating money differently based on its source or intended use. Violates fungibility of money.
The Market Cycle of Emotions
 Optimism ‚Üí Excitement ‚Üí Thrill ‚Üí Euphoria (market top)
 Anxiety ‚Üí Denial ‚Üí Fear ‚Üí Desperation ‚Üí Panic
 Capitulation ‚Üí Despondency ‚Üí Depression (market bottom)
 Hope ‚Üí Relief ‚Üí Optimism (recovery begins)

Chapter 15 ‚Äî Real-World Case Studies
Case studies distill complex market events into actionable lessons. Each case below highlights a different facet
of market dynamics.
Case Study 1: Warren Buffett & Coca-Cola (1988)
Buffett purchased $1B of Coca-Cola in 1988‚Äì1989 at ~15x earnings. Thesis: strong brand moat, global
expansion potential, consistent free cash flow. By 2024, the position is worth ~$25B with $700M+ annual
dividends. Lesson: identify durable competitive advantages and hold through volatility.
Case Study 2: Harshad Mehta Scam (India, 1992)
Broker Harshad Mehta exploited loopholes in bank receipt (BR) system to divert ~Rs 4,000 crore from banks to
stock market. SENSEX rose 143% then crashed 72%. Led to SEBI strengthening, dematerialization of shares,
and banking reforms. Lesson: regulatory gaps create systemic risk.
Case Study 3: Enron Collapse (2001)
Enron used special purpose entities (SPEs) to hide $1B+ in debt off-balance-sheet. Stock fell from $90 to
$0.67. 20,000 employees lost jobs and pensions. Led to Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) requiring CEO/CFO to
personally certify financials. Lesson: analyze financial footnotes; be skeptical of complex accounting.
Case Study 4: Tesla's Meteoric Rise (2019‚Äì2021)
Tesla rose 1,000%+ driven by EV adoption narrative, S&P; 500 inclusion, retail investor enthusiasm, and
short-squeeze dynamics. P/E reached 1,000x. Demonstrated power of narrative and momentum investing. Also
showed risk: stock fell 65% in 2022. Lesson: sentiment can sustain irrational valuations temporarily.
Case Study 5: Infosys ‚Äî India's IT Growth Story
Infosys went public at Rs 95 in 1993. By 2000 it reached Rs 9,000+ (split-adjusted). US listing on NASDAQ
1999. Revenue grew from $2M (1991) to $18B+ (2024). Lesson: quality management, scalable business
model, and demographic tailwinds compound over decades.
Case Study 6: GameStop Short Squeeze (2021)
Reddit community r/WallStreetBets coordinated buying of heavily-shorted GameStop (GME). Stock rose from
$20 to $483 in weeks. Short-sellers (Melvin Capital) lost billions. Robinhood restricted buying amid
clearing-house capital requirements. Lesson: social media can overwhelm institutional positions; liquidity
constraints matter.
